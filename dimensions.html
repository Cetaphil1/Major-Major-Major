01The instrument

Eight signals behind
every "should I switch?"

Students rarely leave a major for one tidy reason. The real causes — money, family pressure, a major chosen for safety, an unclear future, isolation, a draining program — cluster into eight measurable signals. Each is rated with short, first-person statements.

Financial pressureCost, hours worked, time-to-degree — read through workload & school fit.
Family & status pressureA major chosen for safety or prestige — surfaces in motivation.
Department culture & professorsCompetitiveness and teaching style — read through school fit.
Feeling trappedStaying in a major you don't value — read through burnout & motivation.
01Surface

Interest fit

Whether the subject itself genuinely pulls you in — before and during college. Real interest is a buffer, but on its own it's a weak predictor of staying.

"The core subject matter genuinely fascinates me, not just the career it leads to."
"I explored this field on my own — reading, projects, clubs — before I had to declare."
3 itemsAgree · 1–5
02Capability

Skill confidence

How capable you feel handling the actual skills your major demands. Low academic confidence — feeling behind your peers — pushes capable students out.

"I feel capable of handling the technical or analytical skills my major requires."
"I often feel underprepared compared to my peers in core courses." reverse
3 itemsAgree · 1–5
03Demand

Workload tolerance

How the real pace and volume of work sits with you — including outside life stress and the hours you have to work. The most common reason "I like it" turns into "I left."

"I keep up with the volume of work without sacrificing my health."
"I feel buried by problem sets, labs, or readings in this field." reverse
3 itemsFrequency · 1–5
04Drive

Motivation

What's actually driving your effort — genuine curiosity, or pressure from family, status, or a major chosen because it felt safe. Quality of motivation predicts persistence.

"I study because I'm genuinely curious, not only to pass or please others."
"Most of my drive comes from pressure to not fall behind." reverse
3 itemsAgree · 1–5
05Direction

Career clarity

How clearly you can picture — and actually want — where this major leads. Difficulty connecting the major to a real future is a quiet but powerful driver of switching.

"I have a clear picture of the kind of work this major leads to."
"I'm unsure what I'd actually do with this degree after graduating." reverse
3 itemsAgree · 1–5
06Context

School fit

Whether your specific program and campus support how you work — class sizes, competitiveness, advising, course access, and cost. Often it's the school, not the field.

"My program's culture and teaching style suit how I learn best."
"The competition in my program leaves me more drained than motivated." reverse
3 itemsAgree · 1–5
07Social

Belonging

How at home you feel among other students in your field, and whether you have a support network. Belonging and isolation are among the strongest predictors of staying or leaving.

"I feel a sense of belonging among other students in my major."
"I often feel like an outsider in my major." reverse
3 itemsAgree · 1–5
08Risk

Burnout & resilience

Your emotional fuel this term — exhaustion, spillover into sleep and mood, and whether you can recharge. High burnout with low recovery is what makes a liked major feel like a trap.

"I frequently feel emotionally exhausted by the work in my major." reverse
"I can still recharge and come back engaged after a heavy stretch."
3 itemsAgree · 1–5
