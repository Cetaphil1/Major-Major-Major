About the Project

Built by THA BIG S, this project asks a slightly more uncomfortable question than "what major sounds interesting?" — namely, whether your major actually fits your motivation, confidence, workload, belonging, career clarity, and environment. Interest is the easy part. Staying is where it gets honest.

S THA BIG S Creator · designer · chronic over-thinker
The creator
01Why I built this

Students rarely leave a major
for one tidy reason.

I kept seeing the same plot twist: people who were genuinely good at their major and still wanted out, and people who loved the subject but were running on three hours of sleep and vending-machine optimism. "Do you like it?" explained neither group.

Sometimes it really is an interest mismatch. But just as often it's burnout, a program that treats sleep as optional, family or money pressure, advising that's technically available, or a degree with no visible exit. None of that shows up on a quiz that only asks what sounds fun — and "the vibes were off" doesn't hold up great at the financial-aid office.

So I built the thing I wish had existed before anyone picked anything: a quiz that reads the eight signals underneath the interest and gives an honest, advising-style read on whether to stay or switch — and, more importantly, why. Think of it as a second opinion that refuses to sugar-coat it.

02The gap I'm trying to close

Why normal major quizzes
aren't enough.

Most online major quizzes take one input — what you like — and hand back a cheerful label. That's the tip of the iceberg, and we all know how that metaphor ends. It misses the students who like their major and still can't sustain it.

One signal in"What sounds interesting?" predicts your major about as well as a horoscope predicts your week.

03Behind the build

Where it came from,
and where it's going.

What inspired it

The iceberg cliché — interest is just the visible tip — plus actual research on why students switch or drop: workload tolerance, belonging, motivation quality, and burnout. I wanted those to feel real, not like a footnote you skim at 2 a.m.

How it reads you

Short, first-person statements turn into eight 0–100 scores. The model watches the tension between them — loving the subject while quietly drowning in it — to flag switching risk and explain the cause. Receipts included.

What I'm still improving

Making the scoring fair across wildly different majors (engineering and philosophy do not suffer identically), writing sharper stay-vs-switch guidance, and validating questions against real outcomes instead of vibes.

What it's not

Not a clinical assessment, not a crystal ball, and not your parents. It's a prompt for a better conversation — ideally with an advisor who actually knows your situation — in language students recognize from their own lives.
